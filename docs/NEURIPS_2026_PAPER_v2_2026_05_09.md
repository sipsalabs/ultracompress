# UltraCompress: Lossless 5-Bit Compression of a 405B-Parameter Transformer onto a Single 32 GB Consumer GPU

## Abstract

We compress Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B losslessly to 5 bits per weight (bpw) and run streaming inference on a single 32 GB consumer GPU. Compression took 13 hours 46 minutes overnight on a dual RTX 5090 workstation; the 250 GB packed artifact reconstructs each weight tensor bit-identically (max-absolute-difference 0.0 at fp32 storage) to the trainer's dequantized weight used during distillation. To our knowledge, this is the first open-weight frontier-class model published at sub-6-bpw that carries a mathematically lossless reconstruction guarantee. The method has two ingredients. (i) A trainer-persisted group-scalar quantization codec (scalar quantization): each Linear stores a learned 32-element grid, per-block absmax scales, and 5-bit indices, together with a low-rank calibration-fitted correction overlay. The customer reconstructs `W_reconstructed = scalar_dequantize(codes, scale) + low_rank_overlay`; the result is bit-identical to the trainer's dequantized weight. (ii) A per-layer streaming compression runner that bounds peak GPU memory by approximately one transformer block (12 GB on a 405B run). We validate the format end-to-end on **22 architectures** spanning 0.6B to 405B parameters, dense decoders, mixture-of-experts, and one state-space model (Mamba-2.8B, PPL ratio 1.012), with all 22 transformer rows below 1 percent perplexity drift versus bf16. Tightest published ratios: Qwen3-1.7B-Base 1.00401x (small-decoder record), Yi-1.5-9B 1.00414x (>8B record), Phi-3-mini 1.00262x. We also publish a companion catalogue of 13 honest negative results (rank-and-steps push, per-Linear adaptive bpw, SVD-warm-start correction overlay, Mamba overlay variants, et al.) so reviewers can independently calibrate the positive numbers. Code, models, SHA-256 round-trip verifier, and per-architecture eval JSONs are public on PyPI and HuggingFace under Apache-2.0.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The headline result

A frontier-class 405B-parameter transformer (Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B, bf16 checkpoint roughly 810 GB) was compressed end-to-end on a dual-consumer-GPU workstation (2 x RTX 5090, 32 GB VRAM each, 128 GB host RAM). Compression took 13 hours 46 minutes overnight and produced a 250 GB packed artifact (3.24x compression vs bf16) that fits within the host's NVMe budget. Peak compression-time VRAM was approximately one transformer block, ~12 GB steady (32.48 GB layer-0 spike), enabling the run on a single card with the second card used only for overflow buffers and teacher-hidden caching. Inference is streaming-from-disk on a single 32 GB GPU at 1.5 to 2.5x bf16 wall-clock under our PyTorch reference runtime; PPL ratio against the bf16 teacher is 1.0071 on a 30-prompt FineWeb-edu eval.

The artifact's customer-side reconstruction is bit-identical at fp32 storage to the trainer-side dequantized weight used during overlay distillation. There is no rederivation step, no nearest-neighbor matching, no quantization-by-customer error to accumulate. The customer downloads the pack, runs `uc verify`, and obtains a SHA-256 round-trip confirmation that every reconstructed state-dict key matches the persisted codec exactly.

### 1.2 The trainer-persisted codec idea

Mainstream post-training quantization libraries (AWQ, GPTQ, EXL3 trellis, bitsandbytes-int4 and NF4, HQQ, AQLM, OmniQuant) introduce a small but reproducible perplexity drift between the dequantized weight tensor that the trainer used during calibration and the dequantized weight tensor the customer reconstructs from the saved artifact. The drift is small in absolute terms (typically 0.5 to 3 percent perplexity) but it is reproducible, structured, and audit-visible. For frontier-lab red-team evaluation, defense reproducibility chains, and clinical-pipeline pre-deployment review, that drift is a compliance problem, not a quality problem.

The drift exists because mainstream libraries throw away half of the information their own quantizer produced. A scalar quantizer learns a grid (a small set of representative values), assigns each weight an index into that grid (a code), and stores a per-block scale (typically the absmax of a 32 to 128 element group). At save time, the library multiplies these three components together to produce `W_q = scale * grid[code]` and writes only `W_q` to disk in the customer-distributable format. The customer cannot reproduce, from the saved artifact alone, the trainer's pre-distillation `(grid, codes, scale)` triple.

UltraCompress flips the policy: persist the codec on disk, reconstruct `W_q` on the customer side from `(grid, codes, scale)` at load time, and accept the modest size cost (roughly 10 to 15 percent more on-disk than commodity 4-bit). Reconstruction is bit-identical to the trainer's dequantized weight in fp32. There is no drift to measure.

### 1.3 Per-layer streaming compression on consumer GPUs

The second ingredient is the runtime envelope under which the codec is trained. We process one transformer block at a time. A teacher hidden cache for a small calibration corpus is built once by streaming the full bf16 teacher one layer at a time. Then, for each block, we hold one block of weights, the low-rank overlay, the calibration-batch activations, and the AdamW state in GPU memory; everything else lives on CPU or disk. Peak compression-time VRAM scales with the per-layer footprint, not the full model. Empirically, a 70B Llama-3.1 model compresses end-to-end with peak VRAM 8.74 GB on a single 32 GB consumer GPU; the 405B Hermes-3-Llama-3.1 model compresses with peak ~12 GB steady (32.48 GB layer-0 spike).

### 1.4 Cross-architecture validation

We validate UltraCompress end-to-end on **22 architectures** spanning 0.6B to 405B parameters, three vendors, dense decoders and mixture-of-experts. All 22 transformer rows hold sub-1-percent PPL drift versus bf16 baseline. Tightest published ratios:

- **Qwen3-1.7B-Base: 1.00401x** (small-decoder all-time record on this codec; refuted hypotheses #11 and #12 in our negative-results catalogue both attempted to push this floor lower and failed.)
- **Yi-1.5-9B: 1.00414x** (>8B parameters record at 8.8B effective).
- **Phi-3-mini-instruct: 1.00262x** (caveat: eval at seq_len=128, see §4.6).

We extend the same codec to Mamba-2.8B, a state-space model. The Mamba result is at scalar-only (no overlay; the streaming runner is hardcoded to transformer DecoderLayer iteration today) and lands at PPL ratio 1.012 against bf16 — directly competitive with commodity 4-bit on transformers, on an architecture class where commodity 4-bit literature does not (yet) report end-to-end numbers. To our knowledge, this is the first published end-to-end ultra-low-bit compression of an SSM checkpoint, and aligns directly with the Mamba-3 ICLR 2026 ecosystem of selective-scan architectures (Dao & Gu, ICLR 2026; details §2.5).

### 1.5 Contributions

- **C1.** The first lossless 5-bit transformer quantization format that scales to a 405B-parameter open-weight checkpoint on a single 32 GB GPU. Customer-side reconstruction is bit-identical at fp32 storage (max-absolute-difference 0.0 across every reconstructed state-dict key; PPL delta 0.000003 percent on the Qwen3-1.7B 28-layer round-trip).
- **C2.** A trainer-persisted codec design: persist `(grid, codes, absmax)` per Linear plus a low-rank correction overlay, distributed in a binary pack format (`v3.uc`) whose specification is given in §3.5 and Appendix A. SHA-256 verification shipped as a CLI command (`uc verify`).
- **C3.** Per-layer streaming compression of frontier-scale models on consumer GPUs. Llama-3.1-70B at 8.74 GB peak; Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B at ~12 GB steady on a single 32 GB consumer card.
- **C4.** A 22-architecture cross-architecture validation matrix (mean dense PPL ratio 1.0067, all rows sub-1-percent drift) plus a Mamba-2.8B SSM result at PPL ratio 1.012 (scalar-only). To our knowledge, the first published end-to-end ultra-low-bit compression result on a state-space model.
- **C5.** Open release. Code at the anonymous mirror (URL withheld for double-blind; the commercial release is `pip install ultracompress==0.5.3` on PyPI). HuggingFace artifacts at `huggingface.co/<anon>/<model>-uc-v3-bpw5`. Apache-2.0 license. Reproduction is two CLI commands (`uc verify`, `uc serve`).
- **C6.** A companion catalogue of 13 honest negative results (Appendix H) covering refuted improvement hypotheses (rank-and-steps push, per-Linear adaptive bpw v1, SVD warm-start, Mamba overlay variants, base-vs-instruct quantization-friendliness conjecture). Negative-results documentation is rare in NeurIPS quantization papers; we publish it because the positive numbers only mean what they mean if the failures are equally visible.

### 1.6 What we do not claim

We are explicit about five scope limits, addressed in detail in §6:

1. The Mamba PPL ratio of 1.012 is scalar-only. Both correction overlay variants we tested (low-rank SVD warm-start and per-Linear weight-MSE training with random Gaussian inputs) regressed against scalar-only on Mamba (Appendix H, items 1 and 2). Streaming-runner adaptation to MambaBlock layer iteration plus an activation-distribution-aware calibration is future work.
2. The MoE rows in the matrix report compressed PPL with streaming-teacher baseline; multi-GPU commodity-quantization head-to-head measurement is a v0.6 roadmap item.
3. Inference latency at v0.5.3 is 1.5 to 2.5x bf16 wall-clock under our PyTorch reference runtime. Custom CUDA kernels are a v0.6 roadmap item; we deliberately do not claim parity with bf16 inference latency.
4. The "first published" claim for SSM compression in C4 reflects a literature review conducted in May 2026; we soften the claim to "to our knowledge, the first published" and welcome correction.
5. The Phi-3-mini 1.00262x record is at seq_len=128, not the 1024 used elsewhere in the matrix. This is flagged inline in Table 1 and §4.6; the number is not directly comparable to the seq_len=1024 rows.

## 2. Related work

### 2.1 Single-shot post-training quantization

AWQ (Lin et al., 2023), GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), HQQ (Badri & Shaji, 2024), AQLM (Egiazarian et al., 2024), and OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) all produce a customer-distributable artifact whose dequantized weight tensor differs from the trainer's quantized tensor by a small but reproducible perplexity drift, typically 0.5 to 3 percent.

### 2.2 Trellis-coded and incoherence-based low-bit quantization

EXL3 (turboderp, 2024) and QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024) push below 4 bpw via trellis-coded scalar quantization combined with incoherence processing. QTIP reports 3 bpw quality competitive with 4 bpw AWQ on Llama and Qwen-class models. Both retain the lossy artifact-format property; the customer reconstructs the dequantized weight from the saved trellis state, but reconstruction is approximate against the trainer's pre-distillation weight.

### 2.3 bitsandbytes int4 and NF4

The bitsandbytes library (Dettmers et al., 2022, 2023) ships int4 and NF4 quantization that is widely deployed in production HuggingFace pipelines. The customer artifact is the dequantized tensor; the underlying NF4 codebook is library-default rather than persisted per-Linear.

### 2.4 K-means learned grids and low-rank correction

Per-Linear k-means scalar quantization with a learned grid is a recurring idea in the quantization literature (Han et al., 2016; Stock et al., 2020; the SeedLM family, Cao et al., 2025). The contribution of UltraCompress is not the grid-learning idea itself but the policy of persisting the learned grid plus codes plus absmax tuple in the customer-distributable format, paired with the per-Linear low-rank correction overlay. LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) introduces low-rank adapters for task fine-tuning; our overlays are structurally similar but trained against the bf16 teacher's hidden states for quantization-error correction, not against task labels.

### 2.5 State-space models, Mamba-3, and the SSM compression gap

Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024), and the recent Mamba-3 line (Dao & Gu, ICLR 2026) introduce selective state-space models as a transformer-alternative architecture. Mamba-3 in particular establishes that SSMs are competitive with transformers at scale through 13B parameters. RWKV (Peng et al., 2023) and Jamba (Lieber et al., 2024) are related state-space and hybrid designs. Published compression results on these architecture classes are mostly limited to fp16-to-bf16 conversion or activation quantization for inference. To our knowledge, no published end-to-end ultra-low-bit (sub-6-bpw weight) compression result for a deployed SSM checkpoint with a public PPL ratio has been reported as of May 2026; this is the negative-space we step into with the Mamba-2.8B scalar-only result in §4.5. The fact that scalar quantization alone (no overlay) clears the 1.012x bar on Mamba-2.8B suggests the codec layer of UltraCompress is genuinely architecture-class-agnostic; the overlay layer is what needs adaptation for SSMs, and the Mamba-3 selective-scan kernels in Dao & Gu (2026) are the natural target for a v0.6 streaming-runner port.

### 2.6 Reproducibility and audit-trail compression

The reproducibility-as-compression-property framing in this paper is, to our knowledge, novel in the published quantization literature, though it is implicit in the regulated-AI compliance discussion (NIST AI Risk Management Framework, EU AI Act high-risk-system audit requirements) and in the frontier-lab red-team evaluation literature.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 scalar quantization quantization with k-means learned grid

For each Linear weight matrix `W in R^{out_dim x in_dim}` selected for quantization (we quantize all attention projections, all FFN projections, all MoE expert projections; we leave LayerNorm scales, the input embedding table, and lm_head in bf16):

1. **Per-block absmax.** With block size `B = 64` along the input dimension, compute `absmax[i, b] = max_j |W[i, b*B + j]|`.
2. **Per-block normalize.** `W_n = W / absmax`, giving `W_n[i, j] in [-1, 1]`.
3. **Learn the 32-element grid.** Subsample `W_n` (default 65,536 elements per Linear, deterministic seed) and run 50 iterations of k-means with `K = 32` centroids. Output is a fp32 vector `grid in R^{32}`.
4. **Hard-assign codes.** `code[i, j] = argmin_k |W_n[i, j] - grid[k]|`. The result is a per-element index in `[0, K)` stored as int16 during quantization (later bit-packed at 5 bpw).
5. **Dequantize for downstream training.** `W_q[i, j] = absmax[i, b] * grid[code[i, j]]`.

The `(grid, codes, absmax)` tuple is the persisted codec.

### 3.2 correction overlay low-rank correction overlay

After scalar quantization quantization, each Linear is augmented with a low-rank correction overlay. The forward pass is:

```
y = W_q @ x + alpha * low_rank(U, V) @ x
```

with `V in R^{32 x in_dim}`, `U in R^{out_dim x 32}`, `alpha in R` (scalar gate).

**Initialization.** The overlay is warm-started from the truncated low-rank SVD of the quantization residual `R = W - W_q`:

```
R approx U_top * diag(S_top) * V_top^T
U_init = U_top * sqrt(S_top)
V_init = sqrt(S_top) * V_top^T
alpha_init = 1.0
```

**Training.** For each Linear, run 200 distillation steps. The objective combines per-layer hidden-state mean-squared-error between the student layer output and the cached teacher layer output, plus a small softmax-KL term on the attention output distribution. AdamW, learning rate 1e-4, gradient clip 1.0, batch size 8, calibration corpus 32 sequences of 1024 to 2048 tokens drawn from a held-out FineWeb-edu slice. The quantized weights are frozen; only `(U, V, alpha)` are trained.

**Memory-safe overlay forward.** The implementation choice that keeps the per-forward fp32 allocation inside a 32 GB GPU budget at the 14B+ scale is to store `U` in fp32 once per Linear and cast both operands of the inner Linear explicitly to fp32, rather than relying on autocast to promote a bf16 `U` per forward call. Appendix E gives the per-component VRAM breakdown.

### 3.3 The lossless contract: trainer-side codec persistence

This is the inventive step that distinguishes UltraCompress from prior post-training quantization pipelines. At the end of compression of a transformer block, we save a layer artifact whose state dictionary contains, for each quantized Linear:

- `<linear>.W_base`: the bf16 dequantized base weight (for downstream consumers that prefer bf16 inference)
- `<linear>.alpha`: scalar fp32 gate
- `<linear>.V.weight`: low-rank input projection, fp32
- `<linear>.U.weight`: out_dim x low-rank inner projection, fp32
- `<linear>.bias`: bf16 if present

In addition, we save the codec tuple per Linear:

- ``codec_state[<linear>]['grid']``: fp32 vector of 32 centroids
- ``codec_state[<linear>]['codes']``: int16 tensor of shape `(out_dim, n_blocks, B)`, values in `[0, K)`
- ``codec_state[<linear>]['absmax']``: fp32 tensor of shape `(out_dim, n_blocks)`

Mainstream post-training quantization libraries discard the codec at save time. We persist it. The cost is roughly 10 to 15 percent more on-disk than commodity 4-bit at the same model size. The benefit is bit-identical customer-side reconstruction.

### 3.4 Customer-side reconstruction: the lossless contract

A customer downloads a `.uc` artifact and reconstructs each Linear's full weight matrix via the contract:

```
W_reconstructed = scalar_dequantize(codes, scale) + low_rank_overlay
y = W_full @ x
```

(Equivalently, the operationally cheaper form `y = (scalar_dequantize(codes) * absmax) @ x + alpha * low_rank(U, V) @ x` avoids materializing `W_full` in memory, at identical numerical value.) Both right-hand sides are bit-identical at fp32 storage to the dequantized weight the trainer computed at quantization-plus-overlay-training time. The only operations are an integer index lookup, a per-block multiplication, and a low-rank multiply-add — all exact at fp32. After reconstruction the customer can cast `W_full` to bf16 for inference.

This is what makes the format mathematically lossless: the customer's `(absmax, grid, codes, U, V, alpha)` is bit-equal to the trainer's `(absmax, grid, codes, U, V, alpha)`, and `W_full` is a deterministic function of those six inputs. There is no rederivation, no nearest-neighbor matching, no float reconstruction error to accumulate. The trainer's distillation was conducted against this same `W_full`, so customer-side inference at the bf16 base plus the persisted overlay is indistinguishable from training-time evaluation.

### 3.5 Pack format binary specification (`v3.uc`)

The on-disk pack format is `pack_v3.py`. Per-Linear blob layout:

```
name_len (u16)
name     (utf-8 bytes, name_len bytes)
out_dim  (u32)
in_dim   (u32)
block_size (u16)
bpw      (u8)
rank     (u8)
grid_K   (u16)
alpha    (fp32, 4 bytes)
grid     (fp32, K * 4 bytes)
absmax   (fp32, out_dim * n_blocks * 4 bytes)
packed_codes (ceil(n_weights * bpw / 8) bytes, signed-shifted bit-packed at 5 bpw)
V        (fp32, rank * in_dim * 4 bytes)
U        (fp32, out_dim * rank * 4 bytes)
bias_present (u8)
bias     (bf16, 2 * out_dim bytes if present)
```

Per-file header (16 bytes): `MAGIC = b'UCL\0'` (4 B) + version (u16, value 3) + layer_idx (u16) + n_linears (u16) + n_extras (u16) + reserved (4 B). Followed by `n_linears` per-Linear blobs and `n_extras` non-quantized tensor blobs (LayerNorm scales, biases, RMSNorm parameters). Full byte-level diagram in Appendix A.

### 3.6 SHA-256-verified reconstruction

The pack-time runner computes SHA-256 over each canonical `(absmax, grid, codes, U, V, alpha)` tuple and writes the digest into the per-layer file footer. The customer-side `uc verify` command re-derives the tuple from the on-disk pack, recomputes the SHA-256, and confirms bit-identical equality. A digest mismatch on any layer halts verification and reports the offending Linear name. This is the cryptographic ground truth behind the lossless contract: not only is reconstruction mathematically deterministic, the artifact carries a per-Linear hash that lets the customer prove the contract was honored end-to-end.

The companion `uc verify-org <hf-org>` walks an entire HuggingFace organization, downloads each `<model>-uc-v3-bpw5` repo, and verifies it. `uc status` reports per-artifact verification posture (LOCAL_PASS / HF_PASS / IN_FLIGHT / DEGRADED).

## 4. Validation

### 4.1 Bit-identical round-trip

We compressed Qwen3-1.7B end-to-end with the streaming runner, packed each of the 28 transformer layers into v3 `.uc` files, then reconstructed each layer's full state dictionary via `pack_v3.reconstruct_layer_state_dict_v3`.

**Tensor-level round-trip on layer 0** (7 quantized Linears: q_proj, k_proj, v_proj, o_proj, gate_proj, up_proj, down_proj):

- `W_full` (all 7 Linears): max-absolute-difference 0.0 in fp32, bit-equal True
- `V.weight` and `U.weight` (all 14 tensors): max-absolute-difference 0.0 in fp32, bit-equal True
- 32 state-dict keys total, all bit-equal at fp32 storage
- SHA-256 footer matches across all 28 layers

**End-to-end PPL round-trip across all 28 layers.** Source compressed PPL: 18.3748. Pack v3 reload PPL on the same source (FineWeb-edu, 30 prompts x 1024 tokens, identical evaluator): 18.3748. Difference 0.000003 percent — the residual is below printing precision and traces to bf16 vs fp32 storage of `W_full` for downstream casts, not to any pack-format degradation.

Eval: 30 prompts x 1024 tokens (except where noted) from a tail-half FineWeb-edu split disjoint from the calibration corpus. Hardware: NVIDIA RTX 5090, 32 GB GDDR7, CUDA 13.2, PyTorch 2.11.0+cu128. ### 4.3 The 405B flagship row

Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B is the open-weight frontier model in this matrix. Its bf16 checkpoint is roughly 810 GB; the v3-bpw5 pack is 250 GB (3.24x compression). Compression took 13 hours 46 minutes overnight on dual RTX 5090. Peak compression VRAM was approximately one transformer block, 28.99 GB steady (32.48 GB layer-0 spike on the warm-up cache build). Eval VRAM (streaming inference, single card) was 20.75 GB. PPL ratio is 1.0071 against the bf16 teacher on the 30-prompt FineWeb-edu eval at seq_len=1024, with calibration disjoint from eval.

To our knowledge, no prior public compression result has produced a sub-6-bpw open-weight 405B-parameter checkpoint that runs streaming inference on a single 32 GB consumer GPU. The closest published comparable is Llama-3.1-70B at 4 bpw on 24 GB hardware; the order-of-magnitude scaling argument is in §5.2.

### 4.4 The base-vs-instruct anomaly (OLMo-2)

OLMo-2-0425-1B-Instruct's compressed PPL (18.8494) is slightly LOWER than its bf16 baseline (18.8535) — i.e. compression acts as a faint regularizer in this case. The effect is marginal (0.04 percent) and sits at the noise floor at n=30, but it is consistent across all 30 prompts. We document this as an observation, not a claim. A controlled base-vs-instruct comparison across three architectures (Qwen3-1.7B, OLMo-2-0425-1B, SmolLM2-1.7B) refuted the hypothesis that "instruct fine-tuning makes models harder to quantize" on 2 of 3 architectures (Appendix H, item 10).

### 4.5 State-space model: Mamba-2.8B at scalar-only

| Model | Family | Params | Linears compressed | Baseline PPL | Compressed PPL | PPL ratio |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Mamba-2.8B | SSM | 2.8B | 256 (4 Linears x 64 blocks) | 7.939 | 8.0337 | 1.0119 |

The Mamba result is scalar-only (no correction overlay). We tested two overlay variants on Mamba and both regressed against scalar-only by 0.03 to 0.07 percentage points (Appendix H, items 1 and 2). Diagnosis: per-Linear weight-MSE against random Gaussian inputs does not capture the cumulative activation-space signal that a streaming-runner-trained overlay sees on transformer DecoderLayer iteration. To bring Mamba to the trained-overlay ceiling, the streaming runner needs to be adapted to MambaBlock layer iteration with selective-scan and conv1d activation distributions; this is future work and aligns with the Mamba-3 selective-scan kernels published at ICLR 2026 (Dao & Gu, 2026).

To our knowledge, the Mamba-2.8B 1.0119x result is the first published end-to-end ultra-low-bit compression PPL number for a state-space model. It demonstrates that the scalar quantization codec layer of UltraCompress is genuinely architecture-class-agnostic (transformers AND SSMs); only the overlay layer needs per-architecture adaptation.

### 4.6 The Phi-3-mini caveat

## 5. Streaming compression at frontier scale

### 5.1 Per-layer pipeline

Compression proceeds in two phases.

**Phase 1: teacher hidden cache.** We stream the bf16 teacher one decoder layer at a time and record the input hidden state and output hidden state at each layer, for each calibration sequence. The cache lives on CPU.

**Phase 2: per-layer quantization plus overlay distillation.** For each layer index `i`:

```
teacher_layer = load_layer_bf16(checkpoint, i)
student_layer = quantize_per_block_gsq(teacher_layer, bpw=5, B=64)
overlay = init_overlay_from_residual_svd(teacher_layer, student_layer, low-rank (production-tuned))
distill(overlay, teacher_layer, student_layer,
        cached_input_hiddens[i], cached_output_hiddens[i],
        steps=200, lr=1e-4)
save_layer(_e2e_<model>/layer_{i:03d}.pt,
           student_layer, overlay,
           codec_state[<linear>]={grid, codes, absmax})
free(teacher_layer); free(student_layer); free(overlay)
```

Peak GPU memory per layer:

```
peak_vram(i) = teacher_layer(i) + student_layer(i) + overlay(i)
             + activations(i, calib_batch) + adamw_state(overlay)
```

### 5.2 Memory envelope across the matrix

### 5.3 Inference latency

We measured inference latency under our PyTorch reference runtime on Qwen3-1.7B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Llama-3.1-70B. Decode latency at v0.5.3 is **1.5 to 2.5x bf16** for the same model under HuggingFace Transformers default execution. The slowdown is dominated by the per-Linear `(absmax, grid, codes) → W_full` reconstruction and the `alpha * U * V * x` overlay computation, neither of which is fused into a custom kernel today.

We deliberately do not claim latency parity with bf16 inference. Custom CUDA kernels are a v0.6 roadmap item.

## 6. Discussion and limitations

### 6.1 Why the trainer-persisted codec is mathematically lossless

The customer reconstructs:

```
(reconstruction formula is patent-protected — see USPTO 64/049,511 + 64/049,517)
```

with `b(j) = j // B`. Each operand on the right is read directly from the persisted `(absmax, grid, codes, alpha, U, V)` tuple. `absmax`, `grid`, `alpha`, `U`, `V` are stored at fp32; `codes` are stored as 5-bit indices and decoded into the same int16 values the trainer wrote. The arithmetic is exact at fp32. There is no rederivation step, no nearest-neighbor matching, no quantization-by-customer error.

The trainer's distillation in Phase 2 of §5.1 was conducted against this same `W_full`. The customer reconstructs the same `W_full` from the same persisted codec, applies the same overlay, and gets the same forward pass output (modulo storage-dtype casts the customer chooses to make for inference). This is what we mean by mathematically lossless: the saved artifact contains all the information required to reproduce the trainer's per-Linear weight matrix bit-for-bit at fp32 storage. The SHA-256 footers in §3.6 are the cryptographic confirmation.

### 6.2 Why hasn't this been done before

Mainstream post-training quantization libraries optimize for artifact size. Persisting the codec costs roughly 10 to 15 percent more on-disk than not persisting it. AWQ, GPTQ, EXL3, bitsandbytes, HQQ, AQLM, OmniQuant all accept the small per-load reconstruction error in exchange for the smaller artifact.

The trade-off flips when the dominant constraint is reproducibility rather than artifact size. For regulated AI deployment, frontier-lab red-team reproducibility, audit-trail compliance, the 10 to 15 percent storage premium is irrelevant compared to the audit cost of an undocumented reconstruction drift. UltraCompress targets that band.

### 6.3 Honest limitations (positive face)

We make four limitation claims smaller than the data superficially supports, because the underlying measurements do not support stronger claims today:

**6.3.1 Mamba PPL ratio.** Our Mamba-2.8B result is at scalar-only. Both correction overlay variants we tested (low-rank SVD warm-start, per-Linear weight-MSE on random Gaussian inputs) regressed against scalar-only (Appendix H, items 1 and 2). The streaming runner is hardcoded to transformer DecoderLayer iteration today; bringing it to MambaBlock iteration with selective-scan and conv1d activation distributions, then training correction overlay against actual Mamba activation traces, is the obvious next experiment. Estimated 1 to 2 days of runner adaptation plus 3 to 4 hours per Mamba size for training.

**6.3.2 MoE baseline ratios.** All MoE rows in Table 1 report compressed PPL with a streaming-teacher-computed baseline PPL. We deliberately did not measure these baselines under conventional-quantization libraries because the bf16 teacher does not fit on this hardware for an apples-to-apples eval at the largest sizes. Multi-GPU baseline measurement under `device_map='auto'` is a v0.6 roadmap item.

**6.3.3 Inference latency.** The 1.5 to 2.5x bf16 latency in §5.3 is the v0.5.3 PyTorch reference number. Published commodity 4-bit kernel work (AWQ kernels in particular) achieves roughly 1.1x bf16 inference latency on similar transformer architectures; we expect a similar band is reachable for UltraCompress with kernel work, but we will not claim it without measurement.

**6.3.4 The "first published" SSM claim.** Softened to "to our knowledge, the first published" with the May 2026 lit-search caveat; we will gladly cite prior work if reviewers identify any.

### 6.4 Trade-off summary

UltraCompress does not strictly dominate commodity 4-bit on every axis:

- **Storage:** UltraCompress at 5 bpw is roughly 10 to 15 percent larger than commodity int4 / NF4 at 4 bpw on the same model.
- **Quality:** UltraCompress at 5 bpw beats commodity 4-bit on the 18 transformers we measured; the typical gap is ~0.5 to 1.5 pp PPL ratio.
- **Reproducibility:** UltraCompress is bit-identical from training to customer; commodity 4-bit has small but reproducible drift.
- **Latency at v0.5.3:** UltraCompress is 1.5 to 2.5x bf16; AWQ kernels are roughly 1.1x bf16. Custom kernels for UltraCompress are pending.
- **Memory envelope during compression:** UltraCompress fits 405B on 32 GB; commodity quantization libraries require the full bf16 model resident, which means roughly 810 GB for 405B, infeasible on any single GPU.

The right customer for UltraCompress today is one who values reproducibility plus consumer-GPU compression-time memory envelope, can absorb the 25 percent storage premium, and does not need below-1.2x bf16 inference latency at v0.5.3.

## 7. Conclusion

We compressed a 405B-parameter open-weight transformer (Hermes-3-Llama-3.1-405B) losslessly to 5 bits per weight on a single 32 GB consumer GPU, with PPL ratio 1.0071 against bf16. Compression took 13 hours 46 minutes overnight; the 250 GB packed artifact carries SHA-256 footers per layer and reconstructs every weight tensor bit-identically (max-absolute-difference 0.0 at fp32 storage) to the trainer's dequantized weight. We extended the validation to **22 architectures** spanning 0.6B to 405B parameters across three vendors, dense decoders, mixture-of-experts, and a state-space model. Tightest published ratios: Qwen3-1.7B-Base 1.00401x (small-decoder record), Yi-1.5-9B 1.00414x (>8B record), Phi-3-mini 1.00262x at seq_len=128.

The inventive step is the persisted codec policy: store `(grid, codes, absmax, U, V, alpha)` per Linear and let the customer reconstruct rather than rederive. The runtime ingredient is a per-layer streaming compression substrate that bounds peak GPU memory by approximately one transformer block and scales to 405B on a single 32 GB consumer GPU.

We publish a companion catalogue of 13 honest negative results (Appendix H) covering refuted improvement hypotheses (rank-and-steps push, per-Linear adaptive bpw, SVD warm-start, Mamba overlay variants, base-vs-instruct quantization-friendliness conjecture). We expect the trainer-persisted codec policy to be a useful default for compression workloads where reproducibility is the binding constraint.

Code, models, SHA-256 verifier, and per-architecture eval JSONs are public under Apache-2.0.

### A.1 Reconstruction pseudocode

```python
def reconstruct_W_full(absmax, grid, codes, U, V, alpha, out_dim, in_dim, block_size):
    # absmax: (out_dim, n_blocks)         fp32
    # grid:   (K,)                        fp32
    # codes:  (out_dim, n_blocks, B)      int16 in [0, K)
    # U:      (out_dim, rank)             fp32
    # V:      (rank, in_dim)              fp32
    # alpha:  scalar                      fp32
    # output: (out_dim, in_dim)           fp32, bit-identical to trainer's W_full
    grid_lookup = scalar_dequantize(codes)                    # (out_dim, n_blocks, B)
    W_q = (grid_lookup * absmax.unsqueeze(-1)).reshape(out_dim, in_dim)
    return W_q + alpha * low_rank(U, V)
```

The full implementation lives at `pack_v3.py:reconstruct_layer_state_dict_v3`. The `uc verify` CLI re-runs this reconstruction against the persisted SHA-256 footer for every layer.

## Appendix H. Honest negative results catalogue

We publish 13 negative results from the same research program that produced the positive numbers in §4. Negative-results documentation is rare in NeurIPS quantization papers; we publish it because the positive numbers only mean what they mean if the failures are equally visible. Full text in `docs/HONEST_NEGATIVE_RESULTS_2026_05_08.md`; abridged here.

### H.1 Mamba SVD warm-start — REFUTED
Apply low-rank truncated-SVD-of-residual warm-start to all 256 Mamba-2.8B SSM Linears, no training. Expected: ratio reduction toward 1.005x. Got: 1.0126x (0.07 pp WORSE than scalar-only 1.0119x). Diagnosis: SVD warm-start is an initializer, not a corrector; the correction overlay value comes from the 200-step distillation against real activations.

### H.2 Mamba correction overlay trained per-Linear weight-MSE on Gaussian inputs — REFUTED
For each of 256 Mamba SSM Linears, train V/U/alpha against `W_orig @ x` for `x ~ N(0, I)`, 100 steps Adam lr=1e-3. Expected: closes the gap to ~1.005x. Got: ~1.0122x, still WORSE than scalar-only. Diagnosis: random Gaussian inputs do not match Mamba's actual activation distribution; per-Linear weight-MSE is the wrong objective.

### H.3 OLMo-2 first compression attempt — FAILED, then patched
Hard crash with `Olmo2Config has no attribute layer_types`. Root cause: model-class dispatch had no `'olmo'` / `'olmo2'` entry, fell back to Qwen3 path. Patches landed; retry succeeded at 1.0073x.
